About project

My role

  • UX-Researcher
  • UX-Architect
  • UX-Designer

Project goal

The goal of the project is to design an interactive assistance system for preparing petitions to government bodies. This system will provide support and examples for novice users, while also offering reliable information to help experienced users take effective and timely action.

Target audience

The target audience for this project is a diverse group of active adults who require assistance in writing appeals to government agencies. Many of them lack knowledge on how to write appeals effectively, and they would benefit from successful examples that demonstrate the best approach.

Key challenge

  • 1. Manage a complex project by setting priorities, defining the scope of work, and establishing criteria for completion.
  • 2. When working with complex content, consider the specific nature of legal information.
  • 3. User experience is unique, so it was crucial to ensure that the proposed solutions meet real needs.

Reseach study details

I have interviewed users to better understand their experiences when contacting government agencies. I have found that many inexperienced users need support and demonstrations to give them confidence. I also found that power users need to quickly evaluate the appropriateness of the appeal, otherwise they worry about wasting time and effort.

Initial design concepts

I paid attention to how the process of transferring information from one document to another can be simplified for the user. I also thought about how to make it easier to compare and contrast different sources. As a key idea, I took a practice from the physical world - when we place two or more documents in front of us. Device screen size is limited. This forces the user to view the content in a linear sequence. How do you find the ability to view multiple documents with minimal or no transitions? Taking as a basis the maximum size of a desktop monitor, I turned to the concept of splitting the screen into two halves, allowing the user to display different elements of content at the same time.

I used paper sketches, showing the main elements of the content in a schematic way. I combined them with each other, creating optimal pairs in terms of information architecture and user journey. Then I rendered them as a prototype. This idea gave three directions for further work:

  • 1. Direct use of "two-page mode" on wide screens
  • 2. The basis for animation on smaller screens - so that related information is placed as close as possible, making it accessible, for example, using gestures.
  • 3. Semantic grouping of content for a more understandable and simple information architecture and navigation on any device.

User testing details

To make it simpler for users to compare documents and transfer information between them, I utilized the concept of dividing the viewport into two halves, particularly in the desktop version. It was crucial to present all the required information easily, while also preventing the interface from being too complicated.

Uninformative tabs

Users have difficulty recalling and locating the essential documents in the tabs within their favorites collection.

Overloaded timeline

The timeline of sends and responses is too complicated and confusing if the request is sent more than once.

Non-obviousness of sending

Users believe that it is possible to edit the text after sending and this leads to confusion.

Conclusion

What I learned

I was new to working on large projects and faced the challenge of designing a well-planned information architecture and user journey. During this experience, I gained valuable insights into organizing and planning my work as I worked independently on various aspects of the service.

Next steps

  • 1. Continue development of the next interface modules in order of priority.
  • 2. Perform usability testing on the upcoming series of add-ons and ensure that they do not conflict with the existing interface elements and the logical structure of the information architecture.
  • 3. Make adjustments based on the results of usability research and outline further task priorities.
